# Writing

Use this file when the user needs to convert partial research results into a paper draft, response strategy, or talk.

## Writing Starts Early

Do not wait for final results. Start writing once these are available:

- problem statement
- one explicit hypothesis
- one trusted baseline
- one partial result worth interpreting

## Claim-Evidence Map

Every paper section should support a claim.

For each major claim, write:

- claim
- why it matters
- evidence
- strongest alternative explanation
- what experiment rules out that explanation

If a claim has no evidence, lower the claim. Do not inflate wording to hide missing support.

## Paper Skeleton

Use this structure:

1. Problem and stakes.
2. Why existing methods are insufficient.
3. Core idea and intuition.
4. Method details only after the motivation is clear.
5. Experiments that verify the specific claims.
6. Failure cases, limitations, and next-step implications.

## Writing Tests

Use these checks:

- Can the contribution be stated in two sentences without jargon inflation?
- Does each experiment answer a question that appears earlier in the paper?
- Would a skeptical reader know what to disbelieve and where to look for evidence?
- Are limitations stated clearly enough that the next project can grow from them?

## Talks and Weekly Reviews

For slides or spoken updates, keep the flow:

1. target
2. current bottleneck
3. current evidence
4. interpretation
5. next experiment

Do not overload updates with implementation trivia unless the bottleneck is purely engineering.
